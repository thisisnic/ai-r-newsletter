# AI Tooling & R Ecosystem Weekly Digest

A week dominated by Datasette infrastructure releases and a handful of worthwhile takes on how LLMs are changing the day-to-day of software development.

---

## LLM Tooling

**[llm 0.32a2](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/12/llm/#atom-everything)** — NA

A pre-release update to Simon Willison's command-line LLM tool. Worth tracking if you run `llm` in scripts or pipelines, as alpha releases in this project tend to surface useful new model integrations and API surface changes before they stabilize.

**[Using LLM in the shebang line of a script](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/11/llm-shebang/#atom-everything)** — NA

A short, practical note on invoking the `llm` CLI directly from a script's shebang line, effectively making LLM calls a first-class part of shell scripting. The approach is simple and the implications for lightweight automation are real enough to be worth five minutes of your time.

## Datasette Releases & Infrastructure

**[datasette 1.0a29](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/12/datasette/#atom-everything)** — NA

Another alpha on the long road to Datasette 1.0. These incremental releases continue to refine the API and plugin surface; if you maintain a Datasette deployment, this is the one to test against.

**[datasette-ip-rate-limit 0.1a0](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/14/datasette-ip-rate-limit/#atom-everything)** — NA

A new plugin that adds per-IP rate limiting to Datasette instances. For anyone running a public-facing Datasette deployment, this fills an obvious gap, especially as LLM-driven scraping makes rate limiting more necessary than ever.

**[datasette-llm-limits 0.1a0](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/15/datasette-llm-limits/#atom-everything)** — NA

Companion to the rate-limit plugin, this one specifically constrains LLM-related endpoints within Datasette. The combination of these two plugins signals a growing awareness that Datasette deployments need production-grade access controls as they get wired up to AI features.

**[CSP Allow-list Experiment](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/13/csp-allow/#atom-everything)** — NA

Notes on experimenting with Content Security Policy allow-lists in the Datasette context. A security-adjacent release note that matters if you embed Datasette in other applications or serve it with a strict CSP header.

**[Welcome to the Datasette blog](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/13/welcome-to-the-datasette-blog/#atom-everything)** — NA

Datasette now has its own dedicated blog, splitting project-specific announcements away from Willison's personal weblog. A minor housekeeping move, but worth noting if you want to track Datasette releases more cleanly going forward.

## Tooling & Utilities

**[inaturalist-clumper 0.1](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/15/inaturalist-clumper/#atom-everything)** — NA

A small tool for aggregating iNaturalist observation data. Niche, but illustrates the pattern of wrapping open biodiversity APIs into something scriptable — the same approach applies to plenty of other data sources.

**[QR code generator](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/15/qr-code-generator/#atom-everything)** — NA

Willison's notes on a QR code generation tool, likely a quick LLM-assisted build. Included here less for the tool itself and more as a representative example of the kind of small utility that now gets built and shipped in an afternoon.

## Commentary Worth Reading

**[Not so locked in any more](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/14/not-so-locked-in/#atom-everything)** — NA

A piece on the changing landscape of AI vendor lock-in. The argument, broadly, is that the proliferation of compatible APIs and open weights has made switching costs meaningfully lower than they were two years ago — a useful counterweight to the standard lock-in anxiety.

**[Your AI Use Is Breaking My Brain](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/11/zombie-internet/#atom-everything)** — NA

A pointed look at how AI-generated content is degrading the experience of online interaction — the "zombie internet" framing. Dry and frustrated in equal measure, and a reasonable read for anyone thinking about content quality signals in an LLM-saturated environment.

**[Learning on the Shop Floor](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/11/learning-on-the-shop-floor/#atom-everything)** — NA

Reflects on how hands-on, contextual learning differs from structured instruction — applied here to software development with AI tools. The analogy to apprenticeship is a bit well-worn, but the practical observations about where LLMs help and hinder skill acquisition are grounded.

**[Thoughts on GitLab's workforce reduction and 'structural and strategic decisions'](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/11/gitlab-act-2/#atom-everything)** — NA

Willison's commentary on GitLab's layoffs and their framing as strategic rather than reactive. Relevant to anyone tracking how AI productivity claims are being used to justify headcount decisions at developer tooling companies.
